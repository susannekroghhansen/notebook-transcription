## 3. MEANING & INTERPRETATION

This page is a sketchnote summary of *The Squiggly Career* by Sarah Ellis (co-authored with Helen Tupper, though only Ellis is credited here). The page communicates a single, coherent argument through its visual and textual elements:

**The traditional career ladder is obsolete.** The staircase illustration, labelled "The Career Ladder," is immediately critiqued with the annotation "100 Hundred Years Old / Assumes Linear" — positioning it as an outdated industrial-era model. The number "100" is circled for emphasis, underscoring how ancient this framework is relative to modern working life.

**The squiggly career is the modern alternative.** The looping, doubling-back squiggly line emanating from the speech bubble is the visual centrepiece of the page. It literally enacts the concept it describes — a career that moves in unexpected directions, loops back, and defies straight-line progression. The motto "I create not wait" reinforces personal agency as the defining attitude of a squiggly career.

**The "Squiggly Swaps" section operationalises the concept.** The lower right section proposes concrete cognitive reframings: swap "Goals" for letting go of "Plans," and instead think in terms of "Milestones, Chapters, Experiments." The wavy diagram with red squiggles over it visually contrasts the rigid flat plane of a plan with the undulating reality of lived career experience.

**The ladder on the lower left bridges the two halves.** By labelling each rung of a traditional ladder (Plans, Steps, Destination, Titles, Promotion) and bracketing them together, the sketchnote makes explicit what the old model values — and implicitly, what the squiggly career invites you to release.

The overall argument: careers are now personal, non-linear, and self-authored. The tools of the industrial career ladder must be swapped for more fluid, experimental frameworks.

## 4. VISUAL STYLE & COMPOSITION

**Medium:** Black fineliner pen for all line work and text; red/coral marker or felt-tip for highlights, fills, and emphasis strokes.

**Colour palette:** Strictly two-colour — black ink and red/coral. Red is used selectively and consistently for: the title banner strokes, the book spine, the staircase risers, the ladder rails, the "Squiggly Swaps" underline, and the squiggly lines on the diagram. This restraint gives the page visual coherence and makes the red elements feel like a deliberate system of emphasis.

**Typography:** Hand-lettered throughout. Titles and section headers use larger, slightly bolder capital letters (e.g. "SQUIGGLY CAREERS," "SQUIGGLY SWAPS"). Body text and annotations use a casual, mixed-case handwriting style. There is no ruler-straight alignment — text follows the natural flow of the illustrations.

**Composition:** The page is divided loosely into quadrants:
- Upper left: book cover illustration
- Upper right: career ladder staircase + critique
- Middle: squiggly line concept + motto
- Lower left: traditional ladder with labelled rungs
- Lower right: Squiggly Swaps section

The squiggly line in the middle acts as a visual spine connecting the upper and lower halves of the page, and a curved connector line links the book cover to the staircase, creating visual flow across the top of the page.

**Background:** Dot-grid notebook paper (visible faint dot matrix), which is a common substrate for sketchnotes and bullet journaling. The dots do not interfere with the illustration.

**Overall aesthetic:** Energetic, informal, and purposeful. The sketchnote style prioritises concept communication over artistic polish — it is clearly made for personal learning and retention rather than presentation. The consistent use of the squiggly line as both a motif and a literal illustration of the book's central metaphor shows sophisticated visual thinking.
