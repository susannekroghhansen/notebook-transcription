This page documents the **Bill of Materials (BoM) structure** as used in an enterprise resource planning (ERP) or manufacturing context — likely SAP or a similar system, given the terminology ("Usage M", "Usage D", "Usage Ys", "Order BO").

### Upper Section — BoM Usage Types

The four bullet points define distinct **BoM usage categories**:
- **Usage M** is the most comprehensive — covering all materials across all product versions (a universal or master-level BoM).
- **Usage D** is version-specific — narrowing scope to materials for a particular product variant.
- **Usage 2** is the **Master BoM** — likely a planning or reference BoM.
- **Usage Ys** is the **production BoM** — the operationally active BoM used on the shop floor.

### Lower Section — BoM Lifecycle Flowchart

The flowchart illustrates the **progression of a BoM through its lifecycle stages**, from conceptual to operational:
- **Model BoM** → initial product concept
- **Development BoM** *(asterisked — highlighted as important)* → used during R&D
- **Engineering BoM** → used during design/engineering phase
- **Production BoM** *(asterisked — highlighted as important)* → the final, executable BoM used in manufacturing, which feeds directly into an **Order BO** (likely a production or manufacturing order)

The asterisks on "Development BoM" and "Production BoM" suggest these are the two most practically significant stages, or the ones being emphasised in the context of the lesson/session being documented.

### Right Table — Regional and Structural Classification

The large table on the right contextualises the BoM in terms of **regional legal compliance** and **material classification**:
- The numbers **29 (Asia/EU)** and **31 (US)** likely refer to BoM item counts or version numbers that differ due to **different legal requirements** by geography (e.g. different component regulations, safety standards, or labelling requirements).
- The two-column split into **Finished Goods** and **Components** reflects the structural taxonomy within a BoM — distinguishing end products from their constituent parts.

### Overall Argument

The page communicates that a BoM is not a single, static document but a **structured, evolving artefact** that changes in type (usage), lifecycle stage (model → production), and regional configuration (Asia/EU vs US). The flowchart and table together show both the **temporal progression** and the **structural complexity** of BoM management in an industrial or ERP setting.
