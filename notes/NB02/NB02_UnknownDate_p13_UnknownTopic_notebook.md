## 3. MEANING & INTERPRETATION

### Overall Argument
This two-page spread documents a presentation or discussion about **LEGO's global supply chain and site network**, covering:
1. **Where LEGO operates** (geographic footprint — factories, stores, regional hubs)
2. **How stock and sales are managed** (B2B webshop, customer sales offices, country-level stock)
3. **How demand is planned** (using iCP/Kinaxis, with long-term and short-term cycles)

### Top Page Meaning
The top page uses a world map as the organising visual metaphor. The "LEGO SITES" banner anchors the page's subject. The map communicates that LEGO has a **global but regionally differentiated** presence — with local factories, regional hubs, and country-specific stock management. The note that "each country has their own stock" and there are "a lot of very small stores" in Asia suggests a fragmented, localised retail model in that region, contrasting with perhaps more consolidated Western markets.

The factory hierarchy diagram (Local → Regional) embedded in the map suggests a **tiered manufacturing and distribution model**, where local factories feed regional hubs, which in turn supply markets.

### Bottom Page Meaning
The bottom page shifts from geography to **planning systems and processes**. The central cloud labelled "iCP – Kinaxis" identifies the software platform used for integrated capacity planning. The distinction between **Long Term (21 months)** and **Short Term (3–4 months)** demand planning reflects a standard supply chain planning cadence.

The speech bubble on the right contains a critical insight: *"it used to be easy – but when we have 2 KPCs it will be difficult to handle stock."* This suggests a transition point — perhaps moving from single to dual mould/production constraints — making planning more complex. The note that "holding is a global capacity" indicates that inventory holding is managed at a global rather than local level, which is a strategic choice with significant supply chain implications.

The "Reaction / Sight / Bricks" cluster likely refers to **sub-modules or product categories** within the planning system, or perhaps planning horizons (reactive vs. forward-looking).

### Visual–Textual Relationship
The map illustrations ground abstract supply chain concepts in physical geography, making the global scale tangible. The speech bubble visually separates a key insight or quoted remark from the factual notes. The cloud shapes for the planning system suggest something less defined or more conceptual — appropriate for a software/process discussion. The ribbon banner creates a clear visual hierarchy, signalling the section title.

- Text size varies meaningfully: larger for labels and titles, smaller for detail annotations
- Some text is written at angles or along curves to follow the map contours

### Composition
- **Two-page spread** with the top page dominated by the world map and the bottom page splitting between a regional map (left) and text/bubble annotations (right)
- The **LEGO SITES ribbon** acts as the visual anchor and title for the top page
- The **speech bubble** on the bottom right creates a strong focal point for the key insight
- Location pins provide visual rhythm across both maps
- The layout is **organic and non-grid-based**, typical of sketchnote style — elements flow around each other rather than sitting in rigid columns

### Aesthetic Character
The overall aesthetic is **functional sketchnote** — prioritising information capture over decorative finish. The blue wash elevates the maps from pure line drawings to something more visually engaging. The ribbon banner and speech bubble are the most "designed" elements, suggesting the note-taker has a practiced sketchnote vocabulary. The page feels like **live notes taken during a presentation**, with the map serving as a pre-drawn or quickly sketched framework onto which information was layered in real time.
