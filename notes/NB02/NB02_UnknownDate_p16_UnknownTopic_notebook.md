## 3. MEANING & INTERPRETATION

### Core Argument
This page documents a case study — almost certainly about the **LEGO Botanical Roses set** — exploring the supply chain and manufacturing challenges caused by unexpectedly high demand. The overarching theme is stated immediately in the header: **LEGO manufactures 20% more bricks than needed**, and the page unpacks *why* this happens and *how* the system responds.

### Narrative Flow (top to bottom)
1. **The Product Context (top):** The Roses set is introduced as a commercially exceptional product — 1 billion DKK in sales, 3% of the global flower market. Despite being a "C Tier Product" (lower complexity tier), it became the **best-selling LEGO set ever**, which was entirely unforeseen.

2. **The Problem (upper centre):** Because no one could foresee the demand, LEGO urgently needed new moulds to produce the specific single brick (a flower stem, 188 of which are in the small box). The "PROBLEMS" banner visually flags this as a crisis moment.

3. **The Planning Response (middle):** The **PHD (Product/Planning/Demand system?)** calculates expected sales, feeding into a **Development BOM** (Bill of Materials), which structures what components are needed and in what quantities.

4. **The Packing System (lower):** The **LPS (LEGO Packing System)** takes over from the BOM, calculating how to split components into bags. The system deliberately introduces an **"overdose"** — extra bricks added for weight accuracy — which is the direct explanation for why 20% more bricks are manufactured than strictly needed.

### Key Insight
The page elegantly connects a business anomaly (20% overproduction) to its root causes: demand unpredictability, the complexity of small-piece sets, and deliberate system tolerances (overdose for weight). The visual layout reinforces this causal chain through directional arrows and nested boxes.

### Left Page Context
The partially visible left page appears to cover related supply chain concepts: **ICP**, **MRP (Material Requirements Planning)** with step-by-step instructions, **SAP transaction codes**, seasonal demand (high/low season, Red Stream), and box quantity planning — suggesting this is part of a broader LEGO supply chain or operations management course/workshop.
