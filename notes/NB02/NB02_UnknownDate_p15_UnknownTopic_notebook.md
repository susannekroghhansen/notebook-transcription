### Core Argument of the Page

This page documents a briefing or workshop session about **Novelty Push Launches** in a manufacturing and supply chain context — almost certainly within **LEGO** (evidenced by the LEGO box illustration, references to bricks, building sets, and DKK currency on the right page). The page captures the key distinctions, processes, and challenges involved when launching a new product at high volume, as opposed to routine replenishment.

### Structural Logic

The page moves from **strategic framing** (top) → **planning inputs** (middle) → **operational complexity** (lower-middle) → **capacity and risk notes** (bottom):

1. **Top section** establishes the context: novelty launches require planning for a large initial quantity (e.g. 50,000 boxes) rather than the normal replenishment cycle of 5,000 boxes. This immediately signals the scale difference.

2. **The ICP cloud** identifies the four inputs required for planning a novelty launch: Forecast, Inventory, Lead time, and Safe Stock Calculation. The cloud shape visually separates this as a conceptual/planning domain, while the "ICP" box labels the process it feeds.

3. **The pre-packing value insight** (42% value increase) is a critical financial observation: once a box is pre-packed, its value jumps significantly, making it costly to undo. The companion note about complexity of resorting reinforces why pre-packing decisions must be made carefully and early.

4. **The BOM document** is the operational centrepiece: it lists the components of a product (box, loose bricks, pre-packs, instruction booklet). The surrounding annotations — "Who owns the BOM," the SAP transaction code arrow, and the connections to Site Planning and MRP — reveal that BOM ownership and system integration are active concerns or pain points.

5. **The bottom notes** add two important operational constraints:
   - Packing lines need 40% extra capacity buffer to handle demand spikes.
   - Moulding (manufacturing of plastic bricks) is not seasonal, creating a risk distinction: Green Stream (low risk) vs. Red Stream (high risk), likely referring to different product or production streams.

### Visual Rhetoric

The use of the cloud shape for the ICP inputs subtly implies these are "fuzzy" or planning-stage variables. The bold arrow pointing to the BOM from "SAP Transaction Code" emphasises that the BOM is a system-of-record object, not just a concept. The LEGO box illustration grounds the abstract supply chain discussion in a concrete, recognisable product. The open question "Who owns the BOM?" written diagonally suggests this was a live discussion point, possibly a gap or accountability issue raised in the session.
