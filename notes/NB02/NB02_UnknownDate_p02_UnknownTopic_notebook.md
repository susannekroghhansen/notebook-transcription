## 3. MEANING & INTERPRETATION

This page documents notes from a UX podcast episode (likely from Nielsen Norman Group, referenced as "NN/g") focused on the distinction between **Product Design and UX Design**, and the concept of **Journey Management** as a philosophy and practice.

### Core Argument
The page builds a layered argument across three visual zones:

**Top zone (the problem space):** The contrast between "Product centric design" and "Journey centric design" is established as a philosophical divide. The "Position & Structure" section on the right introduces an organisational challenge: designers often have "no mandate" and struggle to connect work "across silos" — a problem visualised literally by the isolated bar chart columns.

**Middle zone (the solution concept):** "Journey Management" is introduced as the answer. The large, perspective journey map is the visual centrepiece and communicates the idea of **longitudinal experience design** — designing not just individual touchpoints but the connected arc of a user's experience over time. The "Top 10 tasks" label and the question "How do we string them together?" frame the practical challenge: how do discrete tasks or touchpoints become a coherent journey?

**Bottom zone (the implications):** Journey Management "brings together business & product" — it is a bridge concept. The two ovals contrast the operational reality ("ongoing research, incremental improvement") with the aspirational question ("How can we be proactively organized around journeys?"). The closing statement — *"We are just looking for what is broken"* — is a self-critical observation, suggesting that current UX practice is reactive rather than proactive, and that Journey Management offers a more forward-thinking alternative.

### Visual Reinforcement
The green highlighting on the journey map peaks reinforces the idea that some moments in the journey matter more than others. The stick figure labelled "intentional" echoes the philosophical shift from reactive product design to intentional, human-centred journey thinking. The bar chart silos visually enact the very problem being described.
